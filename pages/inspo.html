WHAT MAKES A GOOD UI DESIGN?

Today's UI design is a multi-faceted field with different perspectives, expertise, and disciplines to consider. To help you begin, consider these 4 pro tips:

1. PUTTING THE USER FIRST

Remember that user interfaces exist for the user, and no one else. So at each step of your process you shuld be asking: "How will this impact the user?" If its anything other than "it makes their life better or easier," then you might want to rethink your choice.

Great UI doesn't just cater to users - it gives users a sense of control. Simple user interfaces that are clean, intuitive, and seamless are easier to use, and won't need a tutorial to understand.

You can achieve this by:

→ Relying on familiar patterns and best practices"

→ Making it easy to undo changes or fix mistakes

→ Removing distractions

→ Ensure feedback to user actions are timely

2. WELL INFORMED

Even if you're a UI pro, UI needs change depending on the needs of its customers to inform your design.

Doing a quick analysis, will help you to understand:

→ Know your target audiences' expectations - If your idea, project, or product (you could be the product you're trying to sell) is new to the market, you'll need to understand what your audience is expecting from your competition. Using a competitor analysis to evaluate top products or those who have already been successful, can help inform you what some key elements are needed to stand-out.

→ Apply UI standards - These are the written and un-written rules of well-made UI, so you can understand the basics and how to elevate. If you don't these yet, don't worry, we hope to get you covered!

→ Test & solve often - Usability testing can usually reveal somethign your initial design left out. This could be a peer-review, or drawing on inspo from other designers and how they managed to overcome the same issue.

3. APPLIES PROVEN PRINCIPLES

The best UI designers follow evolving standards for accessibl and inclusive design and open design processes - this includes considering new tech, changes in user needs, and ensuring transparency and collaboration when you can.

Stay up to date on content accessibility guidelines here!

"UI design is constantly evolving, particularly with things like text, colour, contrast, or tabbing order."

- Hugo Raymond, Designer Advocate at Figma

COMPARE & CONTRAST

Sometimes it can be tough to grasp the theory of things. Here's a demonstration, using the same resume, of applying good design theories: [hover over the resumes to reveal notes]

Poor and inconsistent alignment

Low contrast between text and background

Colour scheme does not suit the audience

Poor readability of text

Clean grid layout

High contrast between text and background, improving readability

Layout is easy to navigate

Consistent typography throughout

The revised résumé (right) applies key UI and design principles that boost readability and visual hierarchy. The original (left) is cluttered, hard to scan, and lacks alignment — a problem when recruiters spend just seconds reviewing a CV. In contrast, the new version uses whitespace to separate content, consistent typography to guide the eye, and strong contrast for easy reading. It reflects Gestalt principles and Jakob Nielsen’s usability heuristics, making info quick to find without overwhelming the reader. Overall, the redesigned résumé not only looks more polished, but more importantly, it functions better by anticipating the needs of its user: time-pressed employers seeking clarity, relevance, and professionalism.
